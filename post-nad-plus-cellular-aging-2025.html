NAD+ and the Science of Cellular Aging: What We Know in 2025

April 2025 · 6 min read · Murilé BioScience Research Blog

NAD+ has become one of the most researched molecules in longevity science. From Harvard labs to major clinical trials, here is a clear and honest look at what the evidence actually shows — and why injectable NAD+ is so different from supplements.

What is NAD+ and Why Does It Decline?

Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide (NAD+) is a coenzyme present in every cell of your body. It serves two critical functions: it is essential for converting food into cellular energy (ATP), and it is a required cofactor for sirtuins — a family of proteins closely linked to longevity, DNA repair, and healthy gene expression.

The challenge is that NAD+ levels decline significantly with age. Research from Professor David Sinclair's laboratory at Harvard Medical School has shown that NAD+ concentrations in tissues drop by roughly 50% between young adulthood and middle age. This decline is thought to contribute to reduced energy, slower cellular repair, and the broader slowdown we associate with aging.

What the Clinical Research Shows

A landmark 2025 review published in Nature Aging — co-authored by Sinclair — summarised safety, bioavailability, and efficacy data from clinical trials on NAD+ and its precursors. The review confirmed that NAD+ supplementation targets key aging mechanisms including mitochondrial function, DNA repair pathways, epigenetic regulation, and sirtuin activation.

Multiple human clinical trials have shown that NAD+ supplementation improves physical performance and endurance, walking speed and muscle strength in older adults, insulin sensitivity and metabolic markers, sleep quality, and skin aging markers. These findings span trials using various NAD+ precursors including NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside).

The Sirtuin ConnectionSinclair's lab at Harvard, which co-directs the Glenn Center for the Biology of Aging, has established that sirtuins — proteins strongly associated with longevity — require NAD+ to function. Without adequate NAD+, sirtuin activity declines and cellular maintenance deteriorates. Restoring NAD+ levels may help reactivate these protective systems.

Why Injectable NAD+ Works Differently from Capsules

Oral NAD+ supplements face a significant bioavailability challenge. The molecule is largely broken down in the gut before reaching target tissues. Research has shown that orally administered NAD+ precursors are primarily processed in the liver, with limited delivery to other tissues where aging-related decline is occurring.

Injectable NAD+ 500mg bypasses the digestive system entirely, delivering the molecule directly into the bloodstream for immediate cellular uptake. This route of administration is why many clinical users report noticeably increased energy, mental clarity, and improved sleep — sometimes within the first session. The difference in bioavailability between oral and injectable forms is significant.

Honest Assessment: What We Still Do Not Know

It is worth being clear: while the preclinical evidence is compelling and human trial data is growing, the large-scale long-term human studies needed to confirm lifespan or healthspan extension in humans are still in progress. The evidence base for NAD+ is strong and expanding — but it is not yet conclusive in the way that, for example, cardiovascular medication trials are.

⚕ Balanced Perspective: NAD+ shows significant promise across multiple trials. However, as with all wellness peptides, results vary between individuals and the protocol should be tailored with healthcare professional guidance. It is not a substitute for foundational health habits.
